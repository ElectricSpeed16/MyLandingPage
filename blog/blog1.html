What My Kids Taught Me About Patience, Balance, and Time

Posted on Sept 19, 2025

When people hear I have four kids, their first reaction is usually a wide-eyed, “Wow, how do you manage?” I smile, because I ask myself the same question sometimes. My eldest is now 12, my second is 10, and my youngest — the twins — are 9. Looking back, it’s been a journey filled with sleepless nights, overwhelming challenges, and now, priceless moments that I wouldn’t trade for anything.

I still vividly remember when the twins were infants. My wife needed to recover, so much of the daily care was on me. Imagine trying to feed one baby while another cried at the top of his lungs — at 2 in the morning — with barely three hours of rest. I became a master of multitasking without even realizing it: bottle-warming in one hand, rocking a crib with my foot, and trying to catch up on work emails when the house finally went quiet. It wasn’t easy. There were days I felt like I was running on empty, but those moments taught me resilience in a way no job ever could.

Fast forward to today, life feels very different. My kids are growing, and so are our relationships. What used to be about diaper changes and lullabies has turned into conversations — real, meaningful conversations.

One of my happiest moments as a dad is when we go out to eat together. Sitting around the table at a restaurant, my kids talk about everything: what they like, what they dislike, what happened in school, or even just silly jokes that make all of us laugh. My eldest daughter, now 12, sometimes surprises me with how mature her thoughts are — I can already see glimpses of the young woman she’s becoming. My 10-year-old son is growing into his own personality, full of curiosity and humor, always with a random question or observation. And the twins, well, they’re still a handful, but hearing them bicker and then laugh together reminds me how lucky I am to witness their bond.

It’s these simple moments that I treasure the most. Going outside for walks, hearing their little complaints about food they don’t like, or listening to them share their dreams — these are the things I hold on to. I know time moves fast. Soon, they’ll be teenagers with their own worlds, their own friends, their own independence. But for now, they still want to sit with me, talk to me, laugh with me — and that means everything.

Here’s my biggest realization: the struggles I went through when they were small — the sleepless nights, the juggling act between work and family — they shaped me. But now, it’s the little moments that shape us as a family. Patience, balance, and time — those are the gifts my kids have given me. And I’ll carry them with me, not just as a father, but as a person.
